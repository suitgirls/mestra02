Crypto Rush ✨

Melissa Estrada, Friday 7/21/23

The game that I made with my classmates, Ipek and Hannah, was a game about encrypting words into the Caeser cipher alphabet. To start off the game, you and another player have to roll a 6-sided dice. Whatever number you roll, is the N value for you to modify into your normal alphabet to make your new ceaser cipher alphabet. You are then given the flashcards that have the new alphabet ready for you already. Once you have your new alphabet, there is a stack of cards that are faced down. These cards have normal words in them for you to make encrypt into the new alphabet that was given to you. You and the player will be writing down as many words you can into a whiteboard. Not to mention, you have a time limit of 5 minutes to write as many words you can and as well as having a point system to each word you write. You will get one point for every letter of the word on the card and whoever gets the most points wins. The challenges on creating the game were having to discuss ideas within my group. At first, this game was going to be a charades type like game. So, we had to discuss for a long time to come up with the game we have now. I believe that we were given a lot of feedback, so I probably would’ve taken that feedback and addressed the problems done with the game. For example, my TA, Tay, told me that it would’ve been more convenient to create a set of rules, so we don’t have to explain them every time someone comes to play the game. This activity allowed me to show my creativity in many ways, technology wise as well!

Genevive Grotjan, American Mathematician and Cryptanalyst

Melissa Estrada, Monday 7/17/23

Genevive Grotjan was discovered in September of 1940 due to her breakthrough. The Army Signal Intelligence Service (SIS) built an analog machine to decipher the Japanese diplomatic system. The project was called “Purple”. She was known to be good at noticing the differences between the ciphertext and plaintext in messages. Which prompted her to be assigned to a project In October of 1943, the “Venona” project, to decipher the Soviet problem. She was hired into SIS by William Friedman in 1939. She married Hyman Feinstein, a chemist at the National Bureau of Standards in 1943. She later went on to serve as a cryptanalyst and research analyst for the government for over 7 years. Later on, she resigned on May 4 1947. After her resignation, she became a mathematics professor at George Mason University. She unfortunately died in 2006 at the age of 93. Her husband, Hyman Feinstein died in 1995 but had established an award in cryptology at George Mason University, in honor of his wife. She was also inducted into the NSA/CSS, the Cryptologic Hall of Honor in 2010.
